Life of Alan Turing:

  • 23 June 1912 - Alan Turing born in London, Maida Vale
  • 1926-31 - Turing attends Sherborne School
  • 1930 - Alan's friend, Christopher Morcom dies
  • 1931-34 - Undergraduate at King's College, Cambridge University
  • 1932-35 - Turing become a fellow of King's College in Cambridge, specialising in quantum mechanics, probability and logic
  • 1936 - Invention of Turing machine
  • 1936-38 - Turing studies at Princeton University and completes his PhD disseration - Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals
  • 1938 - Turing works with the GC&CS, the British code breaking organisation
  • 1939 - Turing works on The Bombe to decipher German code
  • 1942 - Alan and his colleagues manage to break more complicated German Naval Enigma system, which led to British victory in the Battle of Atlantic
  • 1943-45 - Turing works as a top level intelligence link with USA
  • 1945 - Alan Turing awarded OBE for his wartime service
  • 1948 - Turing becomes a Reader in the Mathematics Department and subsequently a deputy director of the Computing Laboratory at Manchester University
  • 1950 - Alan publishes 'Computing Machinery and Intelligence' in which he develops the Turing Test
  • 1952 - Alan is arrested for gross indecency and subsequently undergoes hormone treatment, which has detrimental effect on his physical and mental health
  • 8 June 1954 - Turing's body is found in his home in Wilmslow, Cheshire
  • 1966 - The annual Turing Award is established and given each year to a person for technical contributions to the computing community
  • 2009 In response to a petition signed by thousands of people, The Prime Minister, Gordon Brown issues a public apology

"Turing was a quite brilliant mathematician, most famous for his work on breaking the German Enigma codes. It is no exaggeration to say that, without his outstanding contribution, the history of the Second World War could have been very different. He truly was one of those individuals we can point to whose unique contribution helped to turn the tide of war. The debt of gratitude he is owed makes it all the more horrifying, therefore, that he was treated so inhumanely."

Gordon Brown, UK's Prime Minister 2009
